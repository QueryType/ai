# Journey of young Siddharth

The antechamber of Prince Siddharth was a masterpiece of curated stillness, a sanctuary where the very air seemed to have been filtered of all impurity. Heavy silk curtains of saffron and deep crimson draped from the vaulted ceiling, their thick folds swallowing the morning light and casting the room in a perpetual, amber twilight. Through the gaps in the fabric, shafts of gold pierced the gloom, illuminating millions of dust motes that danced in a slow, suffocating waltz. A faint, electric hum emanated from the concealed vents of the air conditioning, a sterile breath that fought a losing battle against the oppressive mid-summer heat pressing against the palace walls.

Inside this gilded vacuum, Siddharth was a storm of jagged motion.

He paced the expanse of the white Makrana marble, his bare feet slapping rhythmically against the stone. The tiles were cool, yet to Siddharth, they felt like ice—cold and sterile, an extension of the curated void he inhabited. He stopped abruptly, his small frame shuddering. A sudden, violent wave of nausea rolled through him, a phantom echo of a memory he couldn't quite name. His stomach churned, not from illness, but from the sheer weight of the warnings he had heard in the hushed tones of the royal sages. *The world is a wound,* they had whispered. *To look upon it is to bleed.* 

Siddharth doubled over, a sharp gasp escaping his lips. He gripped the edge of a mahogany side table, his knuckles white, as a small, involuntary shudder wracked his shoulders. He felt the phantom scent of ozone and decay, a stark contrast to the cloying aroma of sandalwood incense that permeated the room. The internal pressure was mounting, a psychic claustrophobia that made the sprawling palace feel less like a home and more like a beautifully upholstered coffin.

The heavy oak doors groaned open with a measured, ceremonial grace. Kumar Adhiraj entered, his presence a study in calculated composure. He carried a silver tray with two glasses of chilled *sharbat*, the condensation beading on the glass like frozen sweat. Adhiraj’s eyes, keen and observant, swept over the room, cataloging the disarray—the overturned velvet cushion, the frantic footprints on the marble, and the trembling boy at the center of the chaos.

From the distance, the palace bells began to toll, their deep, resonant chimes echoing through the corridors. To any other resident of Bilkanshahar, the sound was a comfort, a heartbeat of tradition and stability. To Siddharth, each chime sounded like a hammer striking a nail into a lid.

Adhiraj stepped forward, the soft click of his leather shoes contrasting with the Prince's erratic movements. He set the tray down on a low table with a precision that bordered on the surgical. He did not rush to the boy; instead, he stood a few paces back, his posture rigid, his voice a smooth, grounding anchor in the midst of Siddharth’s volatility.

"The morning air is particularly heavy today, Your Highness," Adhiraj remarked, his tone devoid of alarm, yet laced with a firm, paternal authority. "Your agitation is only fueling the heat. Please, drink. The mint will settle your nerves."

Siddharth did not look at the drink. He turned his gaze toward the curtains, his eyes wide and electric, reflecting a hunger that no amount of royal luxury could sate. He could almost feel it—the vibration of a world that breathed, screamed, and decayed just beyond the marble perimeter. He thought of the words he had scavenged from forbidden texts and hushed servants: *trains* that tore through the landscape like iron serpents, *mountains* that touched the heavens, and *markets* where a thousand colors collided in a chaotic symphony.

He felt another surge of nausea, a physical rejection of the stillness around him. The silence of the room was no longer peaceful; it was an entity, a living, suffocating weight that pressed against his chest, demanding his submission.


Siddharth spun around, the movement so sudden it whipped the air. He didn't want the *sharbat*; he didn't want the mint, the sandalwood, or the curated silence. He lunged toward Adhiraj, his voice breaking the sterile quiet of the antechamber like a stone shattering a mirror.

"Where do they go?" Siddharth demanded. The question was a burst, jagged and raw. "The people, Adhiraj! The ones who aren't here. Where do they live when they aren't polishing these floors or tending to the golden gardens?"

Adhiraj didn't flinch. He remained a pillar of stillness, his expression a mask of patient indulgence, though a small, sharp flicker of concern crossed his eyes. "The world is vast, Your Highness, and largely irrelevant to the duties of the throne. The people serve the crown from their respective provinces—"

"Do not give me the liturgy of the court!" Siddharth interrupted, his voice rising, oscillating between a child’s plea and a young man’s fury. He began to pace again, his gestures becoming expansive, poetic, and frantic. "I have heard things. Whispers in the corridors. Fragments of truth that leaked through the cracks of this... this museum!"

He stopped, his chest heaving, eyes wide with a terrifying brilliance. "I know of the cinema halls, Adhiraj. Halls that make this entire antechamber look like a doll’s house, where light creates worlds on walls as tall as mountains. Real mountains! Not the manicured hills of the royal estate, but peaks that tear into the sky, draped in clouds and ice. And the trains..." He paused, a shudder of genuine thrill running through him. "The iron beasts that roar through the plains, carrying thousands of souls in their bellies, screaming across the earth at speeds that would make the palace horses collapse."

The silence that followed was heavy, charged with the electricity of Siddharth’s revelation. For the first time, the composure of Kumar Adhiraj wavered. He looked at the Prince not as a pupil to be molded, but as a breach in a dam. A shadow of genuine astonishment clouded his features.

"Cinema halls... trains," Adhiraj repeated softly, the words sounding foreign in the hushed air of the room. He sounded less like a tutor and more like a strategist realizing he had missed a critical flank. "Such... vulgarities. Such chaotic notions."

Adhiraj turned slightly, his gaze drifting toward the heavy doors, as if he could see the invisible threads of information that had bypassed his vigilance. The palace was a fortress of ignorance, and Adhiraj was its chief architect. He had spent years pruning the Prince's environment, cutting away the weeds of the 'outside' to ensure Siddharth remained a pure, untouched vessel for the crown.

"How?" Adhiraj murmured, almost to himself. "The librarians are vetted. The tutors are sworn. Even the servants are kept to the outer courts." 

He looked back at Siddharth, his voice regaining its measured, authoritative cadence, though a hint of steel now underpinned the softness. "It seems the merchants from the southern trade guilds have been too talkative during their deliveries. Or perhaps a stray page from a forbidden periodical found its way into your hands. These are distortions, Siddharth. Romanticized fragments of a world that is not as luminous as you imagine it."

"I don't care if it's a distortion!" Siddharth shouted, the sound echoing off the marble walls. "I would rather live in a distortion than in a dream that feels like a cage! Tell me the truth, Adhiraj. Tell me that the world is as loud and terrifying and magnificent as I think it is."

Siddharth’s hands were trembling now, not from fear, but from an agonizing anticipation. He felt the nausea return, a cold knot in his gut that whispered of the sages' warnings, but the hunger for the 'other' was stronger. The palace, with its air-conditioned perfection and its golden gardens, suddenly felt like a suffocating shroud. He looked at Adhiraj—the man who knew everything and told him nothing—and felt a sudden, piercing hatred for the coolness of the room.


Adhiraj sighed, a sound of profound weariness that seemed to age him a decade. He stepped closer to Siddharth, his voice dropping an octave, shifting from the tone of a tutor to that of a guardian warning a child of a precipice.

"You speak of roar and light, Your Highness, but you forget the breath of the season," Adhiraj said, his words slow and deliberate. "Outside these walls, the summer is not a season; it is a predator. The heat does not merely warm; it consumes. It is a white, blinding glare that peels the skin from the bone. The dust storms of Tithagarh are not mere wind—they are walls of grit that choke the lungs and blind the eyes until you are stumbling in a brown void, unable to tell the earth from the sky."

Siddharth opened his mouth to protest, but Adhiraj pressed on, his descriptions becoming vividly, almost violently, visceral. "Men collapse in the streets, their blood thickening in their veins, their minds fracturing under the weight of a sun that shows no mercy. You see a cinema hall; I see the filth of the gutters that lead to it. You see a train; I see the crushing weight of a thousand desperate bodies pressed together in a humid, suffocating swarm. To step out now is not an adventure, Siddharth. It is a descent into a fever dream from which many do not wake."

Siddharth froze. For a moment, the sages' warnings surged back—the nausea, the trembling, the instinctive fear of the *wound*. He felt a cold sweat break across his brow despite the air conditioning. But as the fear peaked, it transformed. The horror of the outside world didn't repel him; it magnetized him. If the world was a wound, he wanted to see the blood. If it was a fever, he wanted to burn.

"Then let me burn!" Siddharth screamed, the sound jagged, a sharp break in the palace's curated harmony. He gripped Adhiraj’s sleeve, his knuckles white, his eyes wide with a desperate, electric hunger. "I cannot stay here! I am suffocating in this silk! I would rather collapse in the dust of a real street than spend one more hour in this sterile tomb! Please, Adhiraj. I beg you. Just once. Let me see."

The silence stretched, taut and vibrating. Adhiraj looked into the boy's eyes and saw a fire that no amount of reasoning could extinguish. He realized that the cage, however gilded, had become a catalyst. If he denied the Prince now, the volatility would only grow into a rebellion. 

"Very well," Adhiraj conceded, his voice a low, defeated murmur. "I will arrange a visit. But it shall be on my terms. Under my protection. And with the utmost caution."

Siddharth let out a cry of triumph, a burst of childlike joy that momentarily erased the shadow of the sages. He spun away, his mind already racing toward the iron beasts and the towering halls.

But as Siddharth paced the room in an ecstatic frenzy, Adhiraj’s expression shifted. The defeated mask slid away, replaced by the cold, calculating gaze of the architect. He did not believe in the Prince's right to the truth; he believed in the Prince's need for a *version* of it. 

As he turned to leave the room to make the arrangements, Adhiraj was already constructing a new cage—one without walls, but made of illusions. He would not take the Prince to the slums or the choking dust of the real markets. Instead, he would curate a "city." He would lease the most opulent shopping plazas, clear the streets of the wretched and the dying, and hire actors to populate the sidewalks with curated joy. He would transform the harsh, vibrant chaos of Tithagarh into a sanitized theme park of materialism. 

The Prince would see the cinema halls, the polished glass, and the roaring trains, but he would see them through a filtered lens. Adhiraj would build a bridge to the outside world, but he would ensure the bridge only led to another room in the palace—just one with larger windows and more expensive curtains. He would give Siddharth the image of freedom, while ensuring the reality remained a distant, whispered ghost.


The transition from the palace to the city was a seamless slide from one dream into another. Siddharth stepped out of the royal limousine and into the atrium of the Grand Imperial Mall, and for a moment, he forgot how to breathe. 

The air here was not the sterile, scentless void of the antechamber; it was a heady, synthetic cocktail of vanilla, expensive leather, and roasting coffee. The architecture was a cathedral of glass and chrome, with light cascading from a ceiling that seemed to dissolve into the blue summer sky, though the heat remained a distant, muted rumor behind the reinforced panes. 

"Look at it, Adhiraj!" Siddharth whispered, his voice trembling with a mixture of awe and an inexplicable, creeping anxiety. 

Everywhere he looked, the world was in motion, yet it felt oddly rhythmic, like a choreographed dance. He saw families walking hand-in-hand, their laughter echoing with a crystalline purity. He saw children on the indoor playgrounds, their shrieks of joy perfectly timed, their clothes pristine and colorful. As they walked, a woman in a vibrant sari approached them with a dazzling smile, offering a sample of a honey-glazed confection. Her eyes were bright, her greeting polished—too polished. 

Siddharth reached out to touch a display of shimmering silks, but his fingers paused. There was something missing. The "noise" he had imagined—the raw, guttural energy of a thousand strangers colliding—was absent. This was not a roar; it was a hum. It was the same hum as the palace air conditioning, just amplified and dressed in neon lights.

"Is it always like this?" Siddharth asked, his curiosity flickering. "Is everyone so... happy?"

"The spirit of the city is one of aspiration, Your Highness," Adhiraj replied, his voice a smooth, guiding current. "People bring their best selves to these centers of commerce."

They moved deeper into the labyrinth of luxury, eventually entering the cinema hall. The scale of it was staggering. The velvet seats were a deep, royal crimson, and the screen was a towering monolith of light. As the movie began, Siddharth felt a jolt of recognition. The imagery—the sweeping vistas, the dramatic crescendos—felt familiar. It was as if he were watching a memory he had been told was a dream. He had seen these stories in the palace archives, and here they were, projected in a space that felt designed specifically to overwhelm his senses. He sat in the dark, the flickering light dancing on his pale skin, feeling a strange sense of vertigo. He was in the 'outside world,' yet he felt as though he were still in his bedroom, merely watching a larger version of the same screen.

The novelty began to sour. The perfection was becoming claustrophobic. The laughter of the children in the food court, the rehearsed kindness of the shopkeepers—it all began to feel like a mirror reflecting a mirror. 

"I want to see the trains," Siddharth said abruptly, standing up as the credits rolled. His voice was no longer a plea; it was a demand for something tactile, something that couldn't be scrubbed clean by a janitorial crew. "The beasts. Take me to the station."

Adhiraj nodded, his expression unreadable. "Of course. The railway awaits."

As they departed the mall, the heat of the mid-summer sun tried to claw its way through the tinted windows of the car. For a fleeting second, Siddharth saw a flash of something through the glass—a man sitting on a dusty curb, his clothes ragged, his face etched with a weariness that didn't belong in Adhiraj's paradise. The man’s eyes met Siddharth’s for a heartbeat, a gaze of raw, unfiltered exhaustion.

Siddharth felt a sudden, violent surge of nausea. His stomach twisted, and he gripped the leather seat, his knuckles whitening. The sages' warnings flared in his mind: *The world is a wound.*

But before he could process the image, the car swept forward, the tinted glass erasing the man and returning Siddharth to the curated silence of the interior. They arrived at the platform, where a pristine, silver-and-blue train sat idling. The platform was spotless, the passengers well-dressed and orderly, waiting in a silence that felt less like peace and more like a held breath.

Siddharth stepped onto the concrete, the heat radiating upward in shimmering waves. He looked at the iron beast before him, but as he leaned in to hear its roar, he wondered why the air still smelled so much like sandalwood.


The station platform was a stage, and for a moment, Siddharth believed the play. He stood before the idling train, the silver paint gleaming under the oppressive noon sun, the air shimmering in heat-haze. He waited for the roar, for the visceral scream of iron on iron that would shatter the curated silence of his life. But the train pulled away with a soft, electric hum, a ghost of a machine designed not to terrify, but to soothe.

It was then that the curtain tore.

Away from the center of the platform, where the polished marble ended and the raw, cracked concrete of the city began, Siddharth saw him. A child, no older than seven, lay curled in the shadow of a rusted pillar. He was a smudge of grey against the grit, his skin a sickly, translucent ochre. Beside him, a woman sat motionless, her eyes hollowed out by a grief so ancient it had become a part of her anatomy. The child’s breath came in shallow, rattling gasps—the sound of a lung fighting a losing battle against the dust of Tithagarh.

Siddharth froze. The nausea that had haunted him in the antechamber returned, not as a flicker, but as a flood. This was the wound. This was the reality the sages had whispered about in the dim light of the temple.

Adhiraj stepped forward, his hand reaching out to steer Siddharth back toward the limousine. "A tragic anomaly, Your Highness. A failure of local administration. Come, the heat is peaking; it is no longer safe to linger."

Siddharth didn't move. He looked at Adhiraj—the measured tone, the flawless suit, the calculated redirection—and suddenly saw the man for what he was. Adhiraj was not a guardian; he was a jailer who had simply expanded the walls of the cell.

"An anomaly?" Siddharth’s voice was a low hiss.

"The world is chaotic, Siddharth. This is why we shield you. This is the 'reality' you craved—decay, disease, and a meaningless end. Does this look like the magnificence you imagined?" Adhiraj’s voice held a note of triumph, a desperate attempt to use the horror as a leash to pull the boy back into the gilded cage.

The spark hit the powder. 

"You lied!" Siddharth screamed. The sound was a jagged tear in the midday heat. "The malls, the cinema, the smiling faces... it was all a lie! You didn't show me the world, you showed me a mirror of your own cowardice!"

He lunged away from Adhiraj, his movements erratic and violent. He began to tear at his own clothes—the fine, breathable silk of his tunic, the embroidered cuffs that marked him as a prince. He ripped a button from his sleeve, the small plastic bead bouncing on the concrete.

"I don't want your protection!" Siddharth shrieked, his face flushed, eyes wide with a terrifying clarity. "I don't want your curated truths! I would rather die in the dust with that child than live one more second in the perfume of Bilkanshahar!"

"Siddharth, be reasonable!" Adhiraj commanded, his voice finally losing its composure, cracking under the weight of the Prince's volatility. "You cannot survive out here! The heat will kill you within hours! You are a child of marble and air conditioning!"

"Then let the sun take me!" 

Siddharth turned and bolted. He didn't run toward the limousine; he ran away from it, sprinting past the bewildered, hired passengers and the stunned security detail. He ran until the silver train was a speck on the horizon, until the scent of sandalwood was replaced by the acrid smell of burning rubber and open sewers.

He stopped at the edge of the station's perimeter, where the road stretched northward, a shimmering ribbon of asphalt disappearing into the distance. Far beyond the smog and the heat-shimmer, he imagined the mountains—the peaks that touched the sky, the ice that could freeze the fever of his soul.

He looked back once. Adhiraj stood in the distance, a small, solitary figure of order in a world that was rapidly dissolving into chaos. The advisor looked fragile, a man clinging to a crumbling script.

Siddharth stripped off his royal jacket and flung it into the gutter, watching as a stray dog sniffed the expensive fabric. He felt a sudden, piercing lightness. The nausea was still there, but it was no longer a warning; it was a compass.

He turned his back on the palace, on the kingdom of Tithagarh, and on the man who had tried to save him from the truth. He began to walk north, his footsteps heavy on the searing road, his skin already beginning to burn under the relentless summer sun. He had no map, no water, and no protection—only a vow.

He would find the mountains. He would find the cold. He would walk until the gilded cage was nothing more than a smudge of gold in his memory.
