# The Ruins of Ashenveil

The air tasted of wet stone and something cloyingly sweet, like rotting lilies left too long in a sealed jar. Lyra Voss pressed herself deeper into the hollow behind a gnarled blackthorn bush, her breath hitching as she watched Brother Aldric through the curtain of falling leaves. He was ten paces from the archway now, his patched grey robe blending with the shadows of the ancient forest floor.

*He doesn't know I'm here,* she thought, though the certainty felt brittle in her mind. *Or maybe he does and doesn't care.*

The fog rolled in from the east, thick and oily, swallowing the tree line whole. It carried a damp chill that seeped through her leather tunic, raising gooseflesh on her arms. The light was failing fast; the low autumn sun had dipped below the canopy, leaving only a bruised purple glow filtering through the branches above. Shadows stretched long and thin across the moss, twisting into shapes that looked almost deliberate, as if the forest itself were rearranging its limbs to watch him pass.

Aldric stopped just before the archway. He didn't hurry. His movements were slow, deliberate, like a man walking through water rather than earth. One hand rested on the hilt of his staff, which was carved from dark wood and bound with silver wire that seemed to hum faintly against the silence.

Lyra shifted her weight, the leather of her boots creaking softly. She kept her face hidden in the shadow of the bush, her eyes scanning him for any sign of recognition. Her shortbow felt heavy across her back, a constant reminder of what she'd become: a ghost haunting her own life. The knife at her hip was cold and slick with old blood, a souvenir from a village fire she still dreamed about sometimes.

*Why does he look so calm?* she wondered, watching the way his white beard caught the dim light. *Three days of tracking, three nights without sleep, and he's just... standing there.*

The smell intensified as he approached the stone. That sweet, wrong scent grew stronger, mingling with the earthy tang of decay. It made her stomach turn, a physical reaction to something unseen pressing against the edge of perception. The archway loomed ahead, its stones blackened by centuries of ash and time. Vines choked the sides, thick as wrists, pulsing slightly in the twilight as if breathing.

Aldric raised his staff, tapping it once against the ground. A low hum vibrated through the soles of Lyra's boots, traveling up her legs and settling in her teeth. The trees around them seemed to lean inward, their branches forming a rough circle over the path.

He turned his head slightly, as if sensing something behind him, but never looked directly at where she hid.

"Is it wise," he murmured, more to himself than anyone else, "to knock on a door that has been closed for so long?"


Lyra stepped from the shadows of the blackthorn, her boots crunching on dry leaves with a sound too loud for the stillness. She kept her hand near the hilt of her knife, fingers brushing the cold metal until it felt like an extension of her own bone. The air around Aldric seemed thicker now, pressing against her skin like wet wool.

"You're late," she said, though he hadn't been anywhere but where she'd left him minutes ago. "Or maybe you just took your time admiring the view."

Aldric turned slowly, his face pale and gaunt in the failing light. His white beard caught the last glimmer of sunset, making him look less like a man and more like a statue carved from frost. He didn't flinch when she revealed herself; instead, he offered her a small, crooked smile that didn't reach his eyes.

"Time is a curious thing," he replied, his voice soft but carrying clearly over the rustle of wind through the trees. "It stretches where we least expect it, and snaps elsewhere without warning. Perhaps I was merely ensuring the door was properly oiled before knocking."

Lyra crossed her arms, feeling the weight of her shortbow against her shoulder. "Oiled? Or just waiting to see if anyone would come?" She took a step closer, keeping him in her peripheral vision while scanning for any sign of traps or hidden dangers. The archway loomed behind him, its stones blackened and jagged, as if they'd been chewed by some ancient beast.

"Why are you here, Aldric?" she asked, her tone clipped. "Three days I've tracked you through mud and briars, past places that make the hair on my arms stand up. You're poking around a graveyard of things the Conclave tried to forget."

Aldric tilted his head slightly, as if considering whether to answer directly. "And why are *you* here, Lyra Voss? A deserter hunting another wanderer in the shadowlands. Surely there's more to your story than just curiosity."

"Curiosity doesn't keep you awake at night wondering what happens when the fog gets too thick," she muttered, glancing back toward the treeline where the darkness seemed to pulse. "Besides, I've got questions of my own. Like why a disgraced monk like you is still breathing after twelve years in the wild."

He chuckled softly, a dry sound that echoed oddly among the trees. "Breathing is not always an act of defiance, Lyra. Sometimes it's simply... persistence. And perhaps a little faith in what lies beyond the veil."

Lyra narrowed her eyes, studying him for any sign of deception. But his expression remained calm, almost serene, as if he truly believed whatever words he spoke were harmless truths rather than dangerous riddles. It unsettled her more than anger ever had.

"Faith," she repeated skeptically. "Right. Because nothing scares you anymore."

"Not everything," he said simply. "Some things... invite themselves in."


The air between them suddenly grew heavy, pressing against their eardrums like deep water. It wasn't a sound they heard so much as felt—a low, rhythmic thrumming that vibrated through the soles of Lyra's boots and up into her teeth. She froze, her hand tightening on the hilt of her knife until her knuckles turned white.

Aldric didn't flinch. Instead, he raised his staff higher, the silver wire binding it glowing with a faint, sickly green light that seemed to pulse in time with the thrumming. The shadows inside the archway stretched outward, detaching themselves from the stone walls and coiling like living things toward them.

"Look," Lyra whispered, her voice barely audible over the rising hum. She pointed toward the darkness within the ruins. "Something's waking up."

"It has been waiting for us," Aldric replied, his tone calm despite the chaos unfolding around him. He took a single step forward, into the path of the emerging shadows. "Not at us. Through us."

The ground beneath them shifted, moss and dirt swirling in a vortex before settling back down as if nothing had happened. A scent of ozone and burnt sugar filled the air, sharp enough to make Lyra's eyes water. The blackthorn vines framing the archway began to writhe, their leaves turning a bruised purple before snapping back to green with unnatural speed.

Lyra drew her bowstring taut, an arrow nocked in seconds. Her heart hammered against her ribs, but she forced herself to keep breathing steady. "What is it?" she demanded, though the question felt hollow even to her own ears. "Another one of your 'living powers'?"

"Perhaps," Aldric said softly. "Or perhaps it is simply remembering that we are here." He glanced at her, his grey eyes reflecting the strange green glow from his staff. For a moment, the distance between them seemed to vanish, replaced by a shared understanding of what lay beyond the threshold. "It does not seek to harm us immediately. Not yet."

"Good," Lyra muttered, though she didn't lower her bow. "Because I was starting to think you'd led me straight into a grave." She scanned the perimeter, looking for any sign of movement or danger. The forest seemed to hold its breath, every leaf and branch still as if waiting for permission to move again.

"Then let us not keep it waiting too long," Aldric said, taking another step forward. "Shall we?"


The thrumming didn't stop; it only grew louder, a heartbeat that seemed to belong to the stone itself rather than any living thing. Lyra's finger tightened on the bowstring, her knuckles white against the dark wood. She could feel the tension in her own muscles, coiled and ready to snap into action at a moment's notice.

"Inside," Aldric said, his voice steady despite the chaos swirling around them. "Or we stand here until whatever is waking up decides we are part of its meal."

Lyra glanced back toward the treeline. The fog had thickened, obscuring the path she'd come from. If they stayed, there was no telling how long they'd be trapped in this liminal space before something dragged them down into the earth. But going in meant stepping blindly into a mouth that hadn't opened for centuries.

"Why should I trust you not to vanish inside and leave me to face whatever comes next?" she asked, her tone sharp but lacking real heat. She knew he was capable of more than just gentle evasion; twelve years of surviving the wild had carved a different kind of strength into him.

"Because," Aldric replied, turning fully toward her now, "if I vanish, you will find your way out eventually. But if we go together, there is at least a chance that one of us might remember what lies beyond." He paused, his grey eyes meeting hers with an intensity that felt almost human. "And perhaps... someone else to ask when the answers grow too heavy for one voice."

Lyra hesitated. Trust was a currency she'd spent recklessly over the years, often paying debts she couldn't afford. Yet here stood a man who had walked through fire and come out smiling, offering her a hand without demanding anything in return. It felt absurd, almost comical, given their history of suspicion and hidden agendas.

"Fine," she muttered finally, lowering her bow slightly but keeping it within reach. "But if you try to lead me straight into a trap, I'll shoot you before the first shadow touches my boot."

Aldric smiled faintly, a gesture that softened his gaunt features. "Then let us hope your aim is as true as your words are sharp." He stepped forward, raising his staff once more as if to guide them through the darkness ahead. Lyra followed close behind, her heart pounding in rhythm with the strange hum filling the air. Together, they crossed the threshold, leaving the safety of the forest behind and stepping into whatever waited within those ancient stones.


The archway swallowed them whole. One moment they stood beneath the bruised sky of the forest; the next, the air inside was still and heavy, smelling of dry dust and something metallic, like old coins left in a damp cellar. The hum that had vibrated through their bones softened into a low thrum, as if the stone itself were holding its breath.

Lyra kept her bow drawn for another heartbeat longer than necessary, her eyes scanning the darkness ahead. The interior of the ruin was vast, far larger than it should have been from the outside. Pillars rose like skeletal fingers toward a ceiling lost in shadow, their surfaces carved with symbols that seemed to shift when viewed from the corner of the eye. Torches flickered along the walls without any visible fuel, casting long, dancing shadows that stretched and retracted against the stone.

Aldric moved forward first, his staff tapping softly on the floor. The sound echoed strangely, not bouncing off the walls but sinking into them as if absorbed by a living thing. He didn't look back at her, though she could feel his presence beside her—a quiet certainty that made her own wariness feel almost absurd.

"Welcome," he said, his voice barely rising above a whisper. "To the place where time forgets to tick."

Lyra lowered her bow slightly but kept one hand near her knife. "Time doesn't forget anything," she muttered, more to herself than him. "It just gets lazy."

She stepped forward, her boots crunching on debris that crumbled into fine powder underfoot. As they moved deeper into the chamber, she noticed something odd: the air around Aldric seemed clearer, less choked with the oppressive weight of the place. Wherever he walked, the shadows retreated slightly, as if reluctant to touch him. It was a small thing, almost imperceptible, but it struck her sharply in the chest.

*He's not just surviving here,* she realized suddenly. *He belongs.*

The thought caught her off guard, unspooling something deep within her that she'd kept tightly wound for years. She glanced at him again, really looked at him this time, and saw not a relic of some forgotten order but someone who had made peace with the unknown. Someone who hadn't fought the dark so much as invited it in.

And then, just beyond the reach of her torchlight, she saw it: a single bookshelf standing alone in the center of the room, untouched by dust or decay. Its pages glowed faintly, pulsing with a soft blue light that seemed to breathe. No one else had seen it before, not even the Conclave scribes who once guarded these halls.

Lyra froze, her breath catching in her throat. She wanted to ask Aldric about it, to demand answers or at least share the wonder of its existence. But instead, she watched as he approached the shelf slowly, his movements deliberate and reverent. His hand hovered over the nearest volume, trembling slightly before making contact.

For a moment, neither of them spoke. The only sound was the faint rustle of pages turning inside the glowing book, a whisper of knowledge waiting to be unleashed. Lyra watched, her heart pounding, wondering what secrets lay hidden within those ancient words—and whether she would ever dare to read them aloud.
