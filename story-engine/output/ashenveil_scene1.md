# The Ruins of Ashenveil

The damp heat of the forest had vanished, replaced by a sudden, biting chill that seeped through Lyra's boots. She crouched low in the loam, her shoulder pressed against the rough bark of a cedar. Ten minutes. She had watched him for ten minutes, her breath hitching in shallow, controlled bursts that barely clouded the air.

Twenty paces ahead, the ruins of Ashenveil waited. 

The entrance archway was a jagged tooth of obsidian stone, half-strangled by blackthorn vines that looked less like plants and more like frozen veins. A thick, rolling fog crept from the east, swallowing the base of the monoliths in a grey tide. It didn't just obscure the view; it carried a scent that made the hair on Lyra's neck prickle. It was the smell of a cold hearth—stale ash and charcoal—layered beneath a cloying, floral sweetness. It was the smell of lilies left too long in a sealed room.

*Funeral flowers,* she thought, her hand drifting instinctively to the hilt of the knife at her hip. The blade was stained, the groove near the hilt darkened by blood that had long since dried into a crust.

Brother Aldric stood at the threshold of the arch, his silhouette gaunt against the encroaching mist. He looked fragile, a splinter of a man in patched grey robes, yet he stood with a stillness that felt deliberate. He wasn't scanning the treeline for threats or checking the wind. He simply stood there, staring into the maw of the ruins with a look of quiet longing.

Lyra shifted her weight, and a dry twig snapped under her boot. The sound was minuscule, a mere click in the vast silence of the woods, but in this place, it sounded like a thunderclap. The ruins seemed to lean inward, the fog swirling faster around the archway as if reacting to the intrusion. 

Aldric didn't turn, but he paused, his shoulders dropping a fraction. He knew someone was there. He just didn't seem to care.


Lyra stepped from the shadows of the cedar, her boots crunching softly on the frost-nipped grass. She didn't draw her weapon, but her hand remained locked on the hilt of her knife, her thumb brushing the worn leather of the grip. She stopped five paces behind him, her voice cutting through the fog like a blade.

"One more step and you're walking into a grave, monk. Though I suppose you've already got the robes for it."

Aldric didn't jump. He didn't even flinch. He turned slowly, his movements fluid and devoid of the frantic energy usually found in men caught by a deserter. His eyes were pale, clouded by age but sharp with a terrifyingly calm intelligence. He looked at Lyra, then at the blood-stained hilt of her knife, and offered a thin, ghost of a smile.

"Is it truly a grave if one enters it willingly?" he asked. His voice was a low rasp, measured and rhythmic. "Or is it merely a doorway to a place the world has forgotten how to name?"

Lyra let out a short, sharp exhale that was almost a laugh. "Right. The 'mystical doorway' speech. I’ve heard it before. Usually right before the person delivering it gets their throat ripped out by something that doesn't like visitors." She tilted her head, scanning the blackthorn vines that seemed to twitch in the periphery of her vision. "The Conclave doesn't ban these ruins because they're 'forgotten,' Aldric. They ban them because they're hungry."

The monk paused, his gaze drifting back toward the obsidian arch. He didn't seem bothered by her presence or the implied threat of her steel. In fact, he looked almost pitying.

"And does the hunger of the stone frighten you more than the hunger of the men you once served?" he asked softly.

Lyra’s expression hardened. The mention of her past hit like a physical blow, though she kept her posture rigid. "I’m not in the mood for a confession, and I'm definitely not in the mood to carry your corpse back to the road. Why are you here?"

Aldric tilted his head, his white beard catching the failing light. "Could it be that I am simply curious if the whispers were true? Or perhaps, is it possible that you followed me because you are hoping to find something here that can wash your hands clean?"

"My hands are fine," Lyra snapped, her voice dropping an octave. "Dirty, but fine."


The silence that followed was heavy, thick as the fog rolling around their ankles. Lyra opened her mouth to deliver a cutting retort, but the words died in her throat. 

From beyond the obsidian arch, there came a sound. It wasn't a scream or a howl, but a wet, rhythmic sliding—the sound of heavy silk being dragged over broken glass. It was accompanied by a sudden, violent shift in the light. The dim grey of the afternoon didn't just fade; it curdled. For a heartbeat, the interior of the ruins flickered with a bruised purple luminescence, a pulse of light that felt less like a visual phenomenon and more like a pressure against the inner ear.

Lyra reacted instantly. She pivoted, her knife clearing the sheath with a metallic hiss, the blade held low and steady. Her eyes darted from the archway to the treeline, her instincts screaming that they were no longer the only hunters in the woods.

Beside her, Aldric’s composure finally fractured. He didn't panic, but he stepped back, his breath hitching. He didn't reach for a weapon—he had none—but his hand flew to the scratched-out symbol on his breast, his fingers clutching the rough fabric of his robe.

"Do you hear it?" he whispered, the formal cadence of his voice replaced by a genuine, trembling urgency. "The resonance. It has awakened."

A low, guttural vibration shuddered through the ground, vibrating up through the soles of Lyra's boots. It felt like a giant heart beating deep beneath the stone, slow and patient. As the vibration peaked, a shape shifted within the fog just past the threshold. It was tall, spindly, and moved with a jerky, unnatural geometry, its form blurring at the edges as if it were being viewed through rain-streaked glass.

Lyra stepped instinctively in front of the monk, her shoulder shielding him. The distrust was still there, simmering, but the immediate threat of the thing in the mist overrode the grudge. 

"Get back," she commanded, her voice a low snarl. She didn't look at him, her gaze locked on the shimmering distortion in the archway. "If that thing comes through, I'm using you as a distraction."

"A pragmatic arrangement," Aldric murmured, though he didn't move to run. He stood close behind her, his presence a strange, steady anchor in the sudden cold. "But tell me, child—do you truly believe steel can cut something made of memory and ash?"


The thing in the mist receded, sliding back into the depths of the ruins with a final, wet shiver. The purple light vanished, leaving the world a bruised, suffocating grey. The silence that returned was worse than the noise; it was a waiting silence, an invitation.

Lyra didn't lower her knife. Her heart hammered against her ribs, a frantic rhythm that clashed with the stillness of the forest. She looked at the archway, then back at the monk. The adrenaline was fading, leaving behind a cold, hard clarity. She knew the layout of these ruins from the reports—the ones the Conclave had buried. Two enforcers had gone in three years ago. They hadn't come back, but they hadn't been dead when they disappeared. They had simply *ceased*.

"We're leaving," Lyra said, her voice clipped. "Now. While that thing is still feeling shy."

Aldric didn't move. He was staring at the threshold, his expression one of profound, agonizing hunger. "And leave the answer to a twelve-year silence? Is that the path you wish to tread, Lyra Voss? To spend the rest of your days running from ghosts because you were too afraid to face the one that calls your name?"

Lyra stiffened, her grip tightening on the knife. "You don't know a damn thing about my ghosts. And I don't trust a man who looks at a death trap and sees a library."

"And I do not trust a woman who carries a blade she refuses to clean," Aldric countered, his voice regaining its formal, distant quality. "It suggests a desire to remember the kill, rather than a need to forget it. We are both broken instruments, are we not?"

The wind picked up, whistling through the blackthorn vines with a sound like a distant choir. Lyra looked at the ruins. She had tracked Aldric for three days because she was desperate for something—a reason, a secret, a way to stop the noise in her head that sounded like burning thatch and screaming villagers. If this disgraced monk knew a way to neutralize the things in the dark, or if he knew why the Conclave feared this place, it was better to have him beside her than chasing him through the brush for another month.

"If you try to lead me into a pit, I'll gut you before we hit the bottom," she said, the words tasting like copper.

Aldric bowed his head slightly, a gesture that was more a formality than an apology. "Would you prefer the alternative? To wander these woods alone, wondering what lay behind the veil?"

"I hate you," Lyra muttered, though she stepped toward the archway, her eyes scanning the fog.

"A sentiment we can both cultivate," Aldric replied softly. "Shall we see if the ruins remember us?"


Lyra stepped forward first, her boots crossing the threshold of the obsidian arch. The transition was visceral; the moment she passed beneath the stone, the air changed. The wind died instantly, replaced by a heavy, stagnant pressure that felt like diving into deep water. The scent of funeral lilies intensified, thick and cloying, coating the back of her throat with a sugary film.

Aldric followed a pace behind, his robes sweeping against the stone. He didn't speak, but his breathing had become shallow, rhythmic, as if he were praying in a language without words.

Inside, the ruins of Ashenveil did not look like a ruin. The walls were not merely stone, but a fused mass of obsidian and something translucent, like frozen smoke. High above, the ceiling had collapsed, letting in a filtered, sickly light that didn't come from the sun, but seemed to leak from the atmosphere itself. The fog they had followed from the forest had followed them in, but here it didn't roll—it coiled, swirling around the bases of shattered pillars like sleeping white serpents.

Lyra kept her knife raised, her eyes scanning the periphery. The silence was absolute, a void that swallowed the sound of their footsteps. 

As she turned a corner past a fallen frieze, her gaze caught something embedded in the translucent wall. She stopped, her breath hitching. 

There, trapped deep within the glass-like stone, was a grey coat. It was a Conclave enforcer's coat, the fabric preserved perfectly in the amber-like grip of the obsidian. The red wax seal on the collar was still bright, a vivid, bleeding drop of crimson against the grey. Beside the coat, a hand was pressed against the interior of the stone—fingers splayed, frozen in a permanent, silent scream of desperation. 

Lyra stared at the dead man's hand, then glanced back at Aldric. The monk was staring ahead, his eyes distant and searching, oblivious to the ghost in the wall. 

She didn't say a word. She simply shifted her grip on her knife and stepped deeper into the silence.
